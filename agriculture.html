What is a GMO?

Genetically modified organisms (GMOs) are living things like plants, animals, or tiny organisms that have been changed in a way that doesn't happen naturally through breeding or mixing genes. This is done using genetic engineering which involves altering their DNA, the genetic instructions that make them who they are.

Advantages of Gentically Modified Organisms

Classical breeding and GM crops differ in altering genes. GM brings targeted changes, while breeding mixes genes randomly. GM ensures specific outcomes, unlike breeding's potential issues. GM's precise control avoids unsafe genes, unlike breeding. For instance, traditional potato breeding can yield toxic substances. GM achieves precise gene changes and new traits without extensive cross-breeding, yet both genetic modification and classical breeding have similar impact on the environment.

How does genetic modification occur?

Creating a genetically modified (GM) crop involves multiple steps, starting with identifying the target gene. This gene is chosen based on its specific trait, like drought tolerance, and can be from the same or a related plant species. Once identified, the gene is cloned and inserted into a construct with a promoter and terminator, then transferred into bacterial plasmids for duplication. The DNA construct is introduced into plant cells through methods like Agrobacterium or gene gun. Only transformed plant cells, identified by traits like antibiotic resistance, survive and are regenerated into whole plants. Genetic analyses are done to ensure the correct insertion and functioning of the target gene, avoiding unintended changes. The transformed plants undergo risk assessments for environmental and health impacts before being introduced to the field.
