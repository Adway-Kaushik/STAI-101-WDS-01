What is CRISPR?

CRISPR, originally a part of bacterial immune systems, has transformed into a precise gene editing tool. Acting as molecular scissors, it cuts DNA at specific sequences under the guidance of customizable directives. Comprising Cas Nucleases and guide RNA (gRNA), CRISPR was discovered in bacterial immunity, where it countered viruses by disabling their DNA. This system employs Cas9 as programmable scissors, guided by RNA to accurately cut DNA at targeted sites. This revolutionary approach accelerates research, potentially curing genetic diseases. CRISPR's value lies in its simplicity, efficiency, and broad applications, from genetic disorders, cancer to drought-resistant crops.

Mechanism of CRISPR

The CRISPR system is a defense mechanism used by bacteria and archaea against viruses. It employs Cas nucleases to cut DNA, creating a memory of viral attacks. When the same virus strikes again, Cas9, guided by gRNA , cuts the virus's DNA at a specific location. Scientists Doudna and Charpentier harnessed this system for gene editing. They adapted it to cut DNA at desired places by modifying gRNA, enabling gene deactivation or insertion. CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing is a game-changing tool that cuts DNA strands and exploits cell repair mechanisms. Two repair methods follow: messy non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), causing small DNA changes, and precise homology-directed repair (HDR), inserting new DNA. HDR's success rate is lower, but researchers are enhancing efficiency through timing and treatments. This technology holds promise for drug development and disease understanding.



How has CRISPR helped?

CRISPR-based tests can diagnose illnesses, including viruses like SARS-CoV-2 and diseases like cancer. They offer cost-effective and simple diagnostic solutions. Additionally, CRISPR technology has facilitated the creation of complex antibiotics through gene editing. This holds promise in addressing antimicrobial resistance and treating neglected diseases, enhancing healthcare solutions.

How did Genetic Engineering help in Healthcare?

Genetic engineering has revolutionized medicine by enabling the production of therapeutic substances like hormones, immune proteins, and blood-clotting factors. Before genetic engineering, substances were harvested from cadavers or donor animals, risking allergies and infections. However, using human DNA to create proteins reduced these risks.

One milestone was genetically engineered human insulin, produced by Genentech in 1982. This technique has been extended to create growth hormones, parathyroid hormones , and interferons used against infections and certain cancers.

Recombinant technology generates cytokines , interleukins , and monoclonal antibodies for antiviral and anticancer treatments. It mass-produces blood factors like clotting proteins and erythropoietin to treat hemophilia and anemia, and tissue plasminogen activator to dissolve clots during heart attacks.

Genetic engineering enhances vaccine safety by inserting protective genes into harmless viruses. Prenatal diagnosis for inherited disorders uses this technology to detect conditions like thalassemias and cystic fibrosis by comparing fetal and parental DNA.

In gene therapy, functional genes are inserted into cells of patients with defective genes. Viruses are common vectors to deliver functional genes into patients' cells, restoring health. This method has shown promise in treating immune deficiencies like adenosine deaminase deficiency.
